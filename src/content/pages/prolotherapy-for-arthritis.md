<div class="wp-block-stackable-heading stk-block-heading stk-block-heading--v2 stk-block stk-8fd2f23" id="how-does-prolotherapy-work" data-block-id="8fd2f23"><style>.stk-8fd2f23{margin-bottom:10px !important}.stk-8fd2f23 .stk-block-heading__text{color:var(--theme-palette-color-8,#ffffff) !important}</style><h2 class="stk-block-heading__text has-text-color has-text-align-left">How Does Prolotherapy Work?</h2></div>

When the concentrated dextrose solution used in prolotherapy is injected, the local cells responds by releasing a burst of growth factors<sup>27-30</sup>. This response from our cells is rapid, with growth factor production rising within minutes to hours of cells being exposed to a concentrated dextrose solution<sup>31</sup>. This surge in local growth factor production is believed to be the cause of the improvement seen in osteoarthritic joints, as well as in other painful, degenerative conditions.

<div class="wp-block-stackable-heading stk-block-heading stk-block-heading--v2 stk-block stk-838acae" id="growth-factors-promote-repair" data-block-id="838acae"><style>.stk-838acae{margin-bottom:10px !important}.stk-838acae .stk-block-heading__text{color:var(--theme-palette-color-4,#192c27) !important}</style><h2 class="stk-block-heading__text has-text-color has-text-align-left">Growth Factors Promote Repair</h2></div>

The main “repair factors” naturally present within joint cartilage, bone and synovial fluid are insulin-like growth factor (IGF), transforming growth factor beta (TGF-β), epidermal growth factor (EGF), basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), and platelet derived growth factor (PDGF). These growth factors encourage the repair process within the joint and surrounding bone<sup>32,33</sup>.

Conversely, interleukin-1 (IL-1) and tumor necrosis factor (TNF) are considered “disrepair factors” within the joint cartilage, bone and synovial fluid. These disrepair factors lead to the breakdown of tissue and limit the effects of the repairing growth factors. IL-1 and TNF have been shown to increase collagenase (which breaks down cartilage) as much as 110-fold and cause a rise in binding proteins (proteins that bind to and inhibit growth factors) in synovial fluid by as much as 24-fold<sup>34-38</sup>. Thankfully, research has shown that dextrose has a down-regulating effect on IL-1 and collagenase<sup>39</sup>.

Animal and in vitro studies have investigated the effects of growth factors on the health of joint cartilage and bone. These studies have found that elevated levels of growth factors within a joint led to chondrogenesis (formation of cartilage cells)<sup>40</sup>, thickening of cartilage<sup>41</sup> and even repair of full thickness cartilage defects<sup>42</sup>.

<div class="wp-block-stackable-image stk-block-image stk-block stk-1ce21c6" data-block-id="1ce21c6"><figure class="stk-img-wrapper stk-image--shape-stretch"><img class="stk-img wp-image-7515" src="/images/charts/prolotherapy-osteoarthritis-graph.png" width="930" height="484" alt="Prolotherapy treatment of knee arthritis"/></figure></div>

The improvements achieved with prolotherapy are typically long lasting. In the longest of all of the studies on prolotherapy, which lasted 2.5 years, the improvement in pain progressively improved up until the end of the study.

The authors of this study stated that “prolotherapy resulted in safe, significant, progressive improvement of knee pain, function and stiffness scores among most participants through a mean follow-up of 2.5 years and may be an appropriate therapy for patients with knee [osteoarthritis] refractory to other conservative care.”<sup>9</sup> In this study improvement increased most rapidly within 10 weeks of the first treatment. Following this initial improvement, a continual progressive improvement was seen up until the end of the study, 2.5 years later.

<div class="wp-block-stackable-text stk-block-text stk-block stk-e97d351" data-block-id="e97d351"><style>.stk-e97d351 .stk-block-text__text{font-size:20px !important}@media screen and (max-width:1023px){.stk-e97d351 .stk-block-text__text{font-size:20px !important}}</style><p class="stk-block-text__text">Hyaluronic acid is another medicine which can be injected into an arthritic joint to help treat the associated pain and disability. For these reasons, hyaluronic acid has been studied and is used clinically as an intra-articular injection for osteoarthritis. </p></div>

<div class="wp-block-stackable-heading stk-block-heading stk-block-heading--v2 stk-block stk-afffbf4" id="safety-of-prolotherapy" data-block-id="afffbf4"><style>.stk-afffbf4{margin-bottom:10px !important}.stk-afffbf4 .stk-block-heading__text{color:var(--theme-palette-color-8,#ffffff) !important}</style><h2 class="stk-block-heading__text has-text-color has-text-align-left">Safety Of Prolotherapy</h2></div>

Prolotherapy is a very safe treatment. The most common issue that can occur is mild discomfort in the joint for 1-7 days after injection. In my clinical experience, discomfort lasts for 24 hours in the vast majority of cases. As with any injection there is a small chance of infection. There is also a small chance of allergic reaction to the local anesthetic (numbing agent) which is used at the site of the injection.
